Brain Made of Logic

A speculative simulation — not a realistic model of the human brain.

The McCulloch–Pitts Neuron

In 1943, Warren McCulloch and Walter Pitts proposed a formal model of the neuron as a logic unit. A neuron receives inputs, sums the excitatory ones, and fires if the total meets or exceeds a threshold θ. Any single inhibitory input prevents firing entirely, regardless of excitatory input.

Their key claim: any logical proposition can be implemented by a network of such units. Thought, they argued, could in principle be formalized as computation.

What This App Does

This app pushes that idea to a speculative extreme. Rather than a single logic gate, it assembles many threshold neurons into subsystems — Perception, Memory, Emotion, Inhibition, Language, Action — and connects them with weighted, signed edges. Stimuli cascade through the network and generate internal states, thought fragments, and behavioral outputs.

The result is theatrical and strange. It is designed to make the original conceptual bridge between neurons, logic, and computation feel vivid — while also making visible what is incomplete and uncanny about it.

Conceptual Grounding

  • Each node follows the McCulloch–Pitts rule: Σ(excitatory) ≥ θ, and no inhibitory input active → fires.
  • Complex behavior emerges from many interacting simple units.
  • Feedback loops create sustained activity, rumination, and spiraling.
  • Parameters change threshold sensitivity, inhibition strength, memory persistence, and noise.

McCulloch, W. S. & Pitts, W. (1943). A logical calculus of the ideas immanent in nervous activity. Bulletin of Mathematical Biophysics, 5(4), 115–133.
